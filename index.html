The Golden Dome Line

A treasure-hunt walk from the mall built over a sailors' village to the street where a golden dome hides a thousand bottle ends.

📏 ~1.4 km ⏱ 1.5–2 hrs 📍 6 stops 🍦 Finish: winner picks the ice-cream flavour

Welcome to Kampong Gelam

Two hundred years ago this was the royal quarter of Singapore — the Sultan's own village, named after the gelam tree whose paper-like bark sailors used to patch their boats. Sailors from Sulawesi, merchants from Yemen, pilgrims from Java: everyone landed here, and every street still tells on them. Kampong means village in Malay — and this walk takes you from a shiny air-conditioned mall back in time, street by street, to a palace gate.

The route is flat, short and packed with things to spot. Start at Bugis MRT, and finish 1.4 km later by a yellow mansion once owned by a prince.

🔍 The Spotter Game

Every stop has a Spotter Challenge. Each one completed = 1 point (adults may not help!). The spotter with the most points at Stop 6 picks the ice-cream flavour everyone else has to order. No swaps. No mercy.

1

Start here · Meet at street level

Bugis Junction — the Mall over a Sailors' Village

You're standing where the sea people lived. The Bugis were master sailors from Sulawesi in Indonesia — so good at trading that people called them the carriers of the East. Every October their ships arrived loaded with nutmeg, camphor and tortoise shell; by the 1830s the Bugis controlled almost all the trade with the eastern islands. In the 1820s a chieftain named Arong Bilawa arrived with about 500 of his people and settled right around here.

The village is long gone, but look up: the old shophouses of Malay, Hylam and Malabar Streets were rebuilt inside this 1995 mall as Singapore's first indoor, air-conditioned streets. You are literally walking through preserved streets — with a glass roof on top.

🔍 Spotter Challenge

Find the Bugis Junction logo — it hides a pinisi, the two-masted Bugis sailing ship. First to point at it (on a sign, door or floor) gets the point.

Stop 2 · Squeeze in

Haji Lane — the Pilgrims' Corridor

This skinny lane is named after the haji — a person who has made the pilgrimage to Mecca. From the 1800s to the 1960s, these shophouses were crammed with pilgrims from Java and beyond, waiting for a ship. Downstairs: agents, money changers and shops selling everything for the voyage. Upstairs: pondok — communal lodges where whole families bunked for weeks. Some pilgrims ran out of money and worked as street hawkers right here until they'd saved enough to sail on.

When aeroplanes replaced pilgrim ships, the lane went quiet and became dusty warehouses. Then artists and tiny boutiques moved in — and painted the whole thing.

🔍 Spotter Challenge

Mural hunt! Count how many animals you can find painted on the walls of the lane (birds, cats, fish — anything alive). Most animals spotted wins. Bonus point: strike a pose copying your favourite mural.

Stop 3 · One street over

Arab Street — Silk, Carpets and Lamps

At the end of Haji Lane you pop out onto Arab Street. In 1822, Raffles' town plan set this quarter aside for Arab merchants, and the street likely takes its name from the Aljunieds — a trading family from Yemen who were among Singapore's very first Arab settlers. They sold textiles, spices and rattan, and they did spectacularly well: by the 1930s, Arab families were among the wealthiest in Singapore, owning whole streets of shophouses.

Look at the shops: bolts of silk and batik stacked to the ceiling, Persian carpets, and mosaic lamps glowing like boiled sweets. Many businesses here have been run by the same families for generations.

🔍 Spotter Challenge

The nose test. Stand still, close your eyes, and name two things you can smell (perfume oils? spices? coffee? incense?). Then everyone votes: which shop window is the most colourful on the whole street?

🧵 Before WWII, Arab families owned huge swathes of Singapore's land

Stop 4 · Best view on the walk

Bussorah Street — the Pilgrim Village

Stop at the top of this street and look: palms on both sides, and the great golden dome dead ahead. For more than a century this was Kampong Kaji — Pilgrim Village. Kaji is how Javanese speakers said haji, because so many pilgrims here came from Java. Until the 1970s, Singapore was Southeast Asia's great pilgrim port: travellers would live on this street for days or weeks, buying supplies and waiting for the kapal haji — the pilgrim ship — to carry them to Jeddah, the port for Mecca.

In 1910 the street was renamed after Bussorah (Basra, in Iraq), along with neighbours like Muscat, Baghdad and Kandahar Streets — a little map of the Muslim world drawn in Singapore street signs.

Count the palm trees lining Bussorah Street — you must agree on one number as a team to score. Bonus point: find a shop sign in a language that isn't English.

Stop 5 · Look up!

Sultan Mosque — the Dome with a Secret

Here it is — Masjid Sultan, the heart of Kampong Gelam. The first mosque on this spot went up in 1824 for Sultan Hussein Shah, helped by 3,000 Spanish dollars from Raffles' East India Company. A century later it was too small, so between 1928 and 1932 it was rebuilt into this golden-domed giant, designed by an Irish architect, Denis Santry, with a prayer hall for 5,000 worshippers.

Now for the secret. Building it cost a fortune, and wealthy donors gave money — but the community wanted everyone to be able to give something. So poorer families donated glass bottles, and the dark ring around the base of each golden dome is made of thousands of glass bottle ends. Look closely: the poorest donors hold up the dome.

🔍 Spotter Challenge

Find the black band of bottle ends at the base of the big dome and point it out to the team. Bonus point: count the small golden domes (not the two big ones) you can see from the street.

Finish · Royal territory

Kandahar Street — a Prince's Yellow Mansion

This last street got its name in 1910, after the city in Afghanistan — probably not because Afghans lived here, but because Persian and Arabian adventure tales were the blockbusters of Malay bangsawan theatre. Its shophouses, built between 1900 and 1940, mix Malay and Chinese styles: check the tiles and shutters.

At the end, by Sultan Gate, stands Gedung Kuning — the Yellow Mansion. It belonged to Tengku Mahmud, a grandson of Sultan Hussein, who painted it yellow, the royal colour of the Malay world. The royal family later fell into debt and the house was mortgaged away — until 1925, when Haji Yusoff, a local rope merchant's son who had walked past it every day as a boy, bought it. His family lived there for four generations. Next door is the old palace itself, Istana Kampong Glam, now the Malay Heritage Centre.

🔍 Spotter Challenge

Final round, double points! (1) Find the yellow mansion. (2) Strike your best royal pose in front of it for a photo. Then tally the scores — the champion spotter chooses the ice-cream flavour for the whole team. Gelato and ice-cream shops are a two-minute walk back towards Haji Lane and Bussorah Street.

Good to know

⏰ Timing. Singapore is hot and humid — walk before 10:30 am or after 4 pm. Haji Lane shops mostly open from late morning; the lane is prettiest (and coolest) early. Golden-hour light on Bussorah Street facing the mosque is superb.

🕌 Sultan Mosque. Visitors are welcome outside prayer times, but hours change (and Fridays around midday are for prayers) — check before you go. Dress modestly; cloaks are lent free at the entrance. Viewing the mosque from outside needs no ticket at any time.

🏛 Malay Heritage Centre / Istana Kampong Glam. The compound has been undergoing a long revamp — check current opening status before promising the kids a museum visit. The buildings are viewable from Sultan Gate regardless.

🚇 Transit. Start: Bugis MRT (EW12 / DT14). Finish: walk ~8 minutes back to Bugis MRT, or ~10 minutes to Nicoll Highway MRT.

💧 Shade & water. Bugis Junction is air-conditioned; Bussorah Street has palm shade; plenty of cafés throughout for cold drinks and toilets.

🍽 Detour idea. Kandahar and Baghdad Streets are full of Malay and Middle Eastern restaurants — nasi padang, murtabak, kebabs — if the walk becomes lunch.
